# Data Story 2026-03-02
## [Americans Are Leaving the U.S. in Record Number](https://www.wsj.com/us-news/americans-leaving-the-us-migration-a5795bfa?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqexlGO2PymmJmu39S8vWx9a6U_d-fkt2-mZrOMx-EDlQTLIdYkrE8UxPsqMmaU%3D&gaa_ts=69a72c41&gaa_sig=hHUwQZ7Nt11lSCas_QNVZbn1nqWi4VwseQLfDxhpvtGoBU5wosmoreNTXB1tXfxiHv7PxF7fmO-PZNBiOcq7xQ%3D%3D)
### The Wallstreet Journal. 02, March

I think this is a very interesting data story. It uses a series of data, such as the growing number of Americans living in European countries and the declining number of Europeans living in the US, to explain the recent drop in U.S. net migration, something that has not happened since the Great Depression.

The story also uses data to argue that calling this trend the so-called “Donald Dash” is a myth. It suggests with data that the decline in net migration is not simply the result of one political moment, but rather a long-term structural trend. The report cites relocation agencies, experts, and immigrants themselves to show that many Americans are moving to Europe, as well as to countries like Mexico and Kenya, because of factors such as economic pressure, healthcare costs, education, and personal safety.

I think the story is very valuable. In some ways, it seems to reinforce the idea that the “American Dream” is fading, or the conception of American Exception is not as truthful as it was. However, I believe the story could have gone further with more data. For example, it could provide long-term data on economic conditions, gun violence, or healthcare costs and compare those trends directly with net migration numbers. That would give readers a clearer sense of possible correlations.

In addition, I don’t think the visualizations were very strong. The chart comparing Norwegians in the U.S. and Americans in Norway was not very clear, since the two lines were too similar in color and therefore not very intuitive to read.
